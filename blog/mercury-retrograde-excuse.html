Mercury Retrograde: The Universal Excuse for Everything Going Wrong

Can't make decisions? Blame the planet. It's what everyone's doing.

I can't make decisions this week because Mercury is retrograde. Nor last week, because the Moon was in Scorpio. Basically, the universe doesn't want me to do anything.

If you've spent any time on social media, you've heard of Mercury retrograde. It's astrology's favorite scapegoat — a cosmic catch-all for bad luck, miscommunication, tech failures, and exes sliding back into your DMs. But what is it really? And does it actually affect your life?

What Mercury Retrograde Actually Is

Mercury retrograde is a real astronomical phenomenon. About three times a year, Mercury appears to move backward in the sky as seen from Earth. It doesn't actually reverse — it's an optical illusion caused by the relative orbital speeds of Earth and Mercury, like when you overtake a slower car on the highway and it seems to move backward.

That's it. That's the astronomy. Now here's what astrology does with it:

What Astrology Says It Means

According to astrologers, Mercury rules communication, technology, travel, and contracts. When it goes retrograde, all of these areas go haywire: emails get lost, phones break, flights get delayed, and you absolutely should not sign any important documents.

Astrology is the only therapy that lets you legitimately cancel plans with cosmic authority. "I can't come to the meeting — Venus is in square with Saturn." Try saying that in a coworking space in Brooklyn and half the room would nod sympathetically.

The Beautiful Logic of Astrological Excuses

What makes astrology brilliant (as a system, not as science) is that it works as a universal operating system for excuses:

  • You're disorganized? It's because you're a Pisces.
  • You're bossy? You're an Aries.
  • You're indecisive? Libra, obviously.
  • You're all three at once? Your rising sign and Moon complicate things.

There's always an explanation. Always.

My Favorite Moment

Someone once told me: "I don't believe in astrology." Then immediately added: "But I'm a Capricorn, and Capricorns are skeptical like that."

The irony was thick enough to cut with a knife, but they didn't see it. Maybe it was Mercury's fault.

What Science Actually Says

Zero evidence. None. Multiple studies have shown that astrologers cannot match birth charts to personality profiles above chance. The Barnum effect — our tendency to accept vague descriptions as uniquely accurate — explains most astrological "hits."

And here's a fun detail: due to the precession of the equinoxes, the zodiac signs no longer align with the actual constellations. If you're "an Aries" according to Western astrology, the Sun was actually in Pisces when you were born. This doesn't seem to bother anyone.

Woo-Woo Meter: 5/5. Full woo. Science has left the building to get a coffee. But it does let you justify anything with "that's such a Gemini thing," which has incalculable social value.
