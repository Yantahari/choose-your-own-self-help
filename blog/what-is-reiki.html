What Is Reiki and Does It Actually Work?

Warm hands, cosmic wifi, and the therapy where someone heals you without touching you.

Reiki is everywhere. Your yoga studio offers it. Your coworker swears by it. Your aunt did a weekend course and now she's a "Level II practitioner" who can send healing energy across continents. But what actually happens in a Reiki session? And does any of it hold up to scrutiny?

The Basics

Reiki is a Japanese practice created in 1922 by Mikao Usui. The word combines rei (universal) and ki (life energy). The idea: a practitioner can channel "universal energy" through their hands to heal your body, mind, and spirit.

In practice, you lie on a table fully clothed while someone places their hands on or near different parts of your body. That's it. No massage, no pressure, sometimes no physical contact at all. The therapist is essentially a cosmic wifi repeater, channeling energy to wherever it needs to go.

What a Session Feels Like

You lie down. Ambient music plays (always with singing bowls or a flute). The therapist places their hands on your head, shoulders, chest, and feet, spending 3-5 minutes in each position. Many people feel warmth or tingling. Others fall asleep. Some feel nothing but leave feeling "lighter." And a few cry — which the therapist considers a sign of "energetic release."

At the end, they always offer you water. Always. As if you'd just run a marathon instead of lying perfectly still for 45 minutes.

The Best Part

Reiki is the only therapy where someone can treat you without touching you, remotely, without you even knowing. It's like the spam of the spiritual world: energy you didn't subscribe to. Should there be a cosmic "unsubscribe" button?

With Reiki Level II, you can send energy at a distance. You sit at home, think of someone, make hand symbols, and beam them Reiki. It's like WhatsApp but without read receipts. The recipient doesn't even have to know. Imagine: you're sitting at home and someone in another city is sending you universal energy without your consent. The GDPR of the energy world would have its hands full.

What Science Says

Science does not support Reiki as an effective therapy beyond placebo. The most rigorous studies (double-blind, controlled) find no significant difference between real Reiki and fake Reiki (an untrained person simply placing their hands near you).

But — and this is an important but — the ritual itself has value. Someone dedicating an hour of undivided attention to you in a calm environment, telling you everything will be okay, has a power that shouldn't be underestimated. It's just not the energy doing it.

Woo-Woo Meter: 4/5. We're airborne. But if it relaxes you, no one's judging. Well, your engineer brother-in-law might.
